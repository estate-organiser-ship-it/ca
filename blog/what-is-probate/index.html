What Is Probate in Canada, and Will Your Family Need It?

Jul 16, 2026

Probate is one of those words everyone has heard and few people can define. Here it is in plain English, and more importantly, what it means for your family.

The short version

Probate is a court process that confirms a will is valid and confirms the executor's authority to act. Banks and land registries often want to see that confirmation before they hand anything over. Every province runs it differently, right down to the name: a grant of probate in British Columbia, a Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee in Ontario.

The cost varies by province

Most provinces charge a fee or tax based on the estate's value, and the rates genuinely differ across the country. In Ontario, for example, the Estate Administration Tax applies above a threshold; in BC, probate fees step up with estate value. What is consistent: assets that pass outside the estate, like insurance and registered accounts with named beneficiaries, are generally not counted.

Is it always needed?

No. Assets with named beneficiaries, jointly held property in most provinces, and anything institutions are willing to release without a grant can skip the process. Some provinces also offer simplified routes for smaller estates. And Quebec has a genuine shortcut: a notarial will does not need to be probated at all, while handwritten and witnessed wills there do.

What the process generally involves

Applying to the court with the will and supporting documents, paying the fee or tax, and receiving the formal confirmation that lets the executor act. It takes time, it generates paperwork, and it goes far more smoothly when the information is easy to find.

How to make it far easier in advance

Keep beneficiary designations current so those assets skip the process entirely. Make sure the will is properly done and findable. And leave one document that maps everything: accounts, policies, property, contacts, password locations, wishes. Probate is paperwork, and paperwork is fast when nothing has to be hunted down. Common questions about probate

How long does it take, and what does it cost?

Both vary by province and by how complicated the estate is. Simple estates can be reasonably quick; contested or messy ones take longer.

Should we use a lawyer or notary?

Many families handle simple estates with their province's own guidance, and bring in a professional when there is real complexity, conflict or doubt. There is no prize for struggling alone.
