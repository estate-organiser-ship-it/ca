Power of Attorney vs a Will: What Is the Difference?

Jul 16, 2026

These two documents get mixed up constantly, and the confusion matters, because each one covers a gap the other cannot.

A will is for after you die

A will says who should receive what you own, and who should be in charge of making that happen. It has no power at all while you are alive, and it only speaks after death.

A power of attorney is for while you are alive

A power of attorney lets someone you choose make financial or personal decisions for you while you are alive. It works the other way round from a will: it operates during your lifetime, and it ends at your death, at which point the will and the executor take over.

The names and forms vary by province, and there are different documents for finances and for personal or health care decisions. In Quebec, the lifetime document is the protection mandate, which names the people who will look after you and your property if you become incapable, and which takes effect through a court process.

Why the gap matters

Picture the in-between: someone is alive but cannot manage their own affairs after a stroke or a diagnosis. The will cannot help, it is not in force yet. Without the right documents in place, even a spouse can hit walls trying to deal with banks or make care decisions, and a court process may be the only path.

What to do about it

Getting these documents done properly is a job for a lawyer or notary in your province. And once they exist, they only help if people can find them. Record what exists, where it is kept and who holds which role in one place your family knows about. That is a core chapter of The Estate Organiser.
